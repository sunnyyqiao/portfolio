Should a “Data-to-Earn” Telecom Exist?

Market-entry feasibility for a two-sided telecom startup

BUSINESS CHALLENGE

Phone bills are a heavy cost for budget users. Companies need bot-free human data. Can a “data-to-earn” model serve both?

MY ROLE

  • Business Analyst
  • Product Designer
  • Solo ~4 weeks

SOLUTION

Verdict: conditionally viable — My recommendation: validate demand cheaply before building.

DELIVERED

  • Market Entry Report
  • B2B Client Strategy
  • User Validation Roadmap
  • Interactive Prototype

Context

Founder's pitch: a wireless plan where users pay with data, not money — completing micro-tasks (AI training, surveys) to earn phone credit, while their verified, bot-free data is sold to companies.

Is it viable — before we begin to build?

As a Product Designer taking on a Business Analyst role, I treated the business model itself as the thing to be designed and tested at the minimum cost, not just designing the screens.

Setting the Hypothesis

Consumers will trade their time and verified identity for wireless credit, and enterprises will pay a premium for bot-free, SIM-verified human data from multicultural, real-world populations. We will aim to lower fixed cost and get quick-wins.

Analytical Framework

Before any money or code went in, is this worth building at all?

I broke it into the three checkpoints:

Three principles ran through the work: treating regulation as a first-order risk, reverse-engineering who the real customer is, and refusing to trust any number I couldn't verify.

Regulations

I prioritized regulations first, as this business is built on collecting and reselling personal data, so if the law doesn’t allow it, the entire model collapses. I mapped these as sequential compliance gates; each must be cleared before moving to the next step.

Furthermore, distilling these into a regulatory risk matrix (likelihood × impact, with mitigations), CPNI and AB5 are the make-or-break risks. CCPA is serious but manageable; COPPA and FTC scrutiny are lower priority.

Demand & Supply

This is a B2B data supply business in a B2C wrapper, so the wireless plan is the hook that attracts the people whose data we sell.

We build a platform to facilitate this value exchange between data contributors and enterprise partners, where payments from enterprise data buyers directly offset consumers' mobile bills under this data-to-earn ecosystem.

The Reframe

The real customers are the enterprises buying that data, and they partially subsidize the phone bill of users as their data producers, so I reverse-engineered the actual demand from who gives the business revenue, and split the market into two sides:

DEMAND: Enterprises that need real market feedback to refine their products and grow sales.

Will enterprises pay for the data?

SUPPLY: People who will produce data with real market value in exchange for billing credit.

Will users do tasks for credit?

Solving the Business Paradox

PROBLEM: The users most eager to save have lower disposable income and little spending power to traditional advertisers.

RETHINK: The resolution wasn't to “find rich users.” It was pick the buyers for whom this exact audience is valuable.

SOLUTION: Target whoever produces data that these specific B2B clients will find essential and pay for.

Client Acquisition Approach

I sequenced enterprise outreach by fit & decision-making speed, using funding round as a proxy.

FIRST TARGETS: Early-stage companies are open to new vendors — better price, room to negotiate, and shorter sales cycle.

We earn revenue early and secure quick wins.

LATER ANCHORS: Large public companies mean long procurement and locked-in suppliers.

Set aside until early wins open bigger doors.

Market Sizing

To see whether the economics work, I sized the market and built the unit-economics model.

Fact-checking each against primary sources, I found:

  • An impossible user count (50M US college students → actually ~15M)
  • A fabricated citation - a “61%” stat from a firm and year that never existed
  • Enterprise rates aren't publicly disclosed at all

I marked every figure from an unverified source as an assumption. The unit economics became a sensitivity model instead of a validated revenue projection. It's a map of which numbers matter most and need testing first.

Key Design Choice

Making the earning loop legible and trustworthy

Because the whole model asks users to share data, transparency of ‘what you've earned and why’ makes the value exchange feel fair.

Outcomes

Verdict: conditionally viable, if execution is disciplined on regulatory compliance, B2B pricing, and user engagement. I also delivered a validation roadmap for what comes next, and below four deliverables will be sufficient for the client to make initial testing and decision. My work helped the founder avoid committing an estimated ~$40K to a premature build.

What I learned

I came away convinced that the business analyst role is about to change shape. The analysts who'll thrive are the ones who can multitask across tools to deliver multi-dimensional analysis, narrate a complex business challenge simply, pairing hyperspecialized industry expertise with the ability to put a testable product in the client's hands early.

We, as human business analyst and product designer, should embrace our taste, observation, and strategic decision-making. We decide what's worth doing, and know what "good" looks like. That's the part AI can't hand us.
